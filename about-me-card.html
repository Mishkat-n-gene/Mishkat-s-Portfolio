Me and Bioinformatics

Back in 2019 when Covid-19 hit the world, I was dazed — how could something as small and minute as a virus make as big an organism as a human fall sick to the point of death? What sort of machinery could a not-quite-living organism like a virus possibly possess? It fascinated me, it frightened me — a 16-year-old girl merely doing her matriculation.

Then in 2020, I contracted the virus twice, with a gap of six months. The same symptoms both times: losing my taste, smell, and hearing to a certain extent. In quarantine, I researched everything I could, read the daily news on websites about a cure for this epidemic, and fueled by my brain and a lifelong fascination with Sci-Fi movies, I built a deeper interest in biological molecules and their capacity to create — and break — the world.

This was when I learnt about Bioinformatics. The interdisciplinary field born at the synergy of Biology and the core principles of a whole lot of Mathematics. Previously, back in 2017, one of my very dearest aunts had died of breast cancer. By then I had plans of becoming a doctor, yet I never stopped imagining doing further research in the cancer domain — and cancer, surprisingly, is a very integral area of study in Bioinformatics today. Then to Covid, I lost yet another aunt. To say the least, this took a deep toll on me, and only strengthened my desire to research and provide better, more accessible solutions that are not only effective but within reach of the everyday person. Mentioning at the very end, the biggest loss of my life was losing my mother early in life to a heart disease. Diseases, they intrigued me, they still do! And what better closure is there to study, research and build for the betterment of humanity! Just so our loved ones are saved.

Driven by my personal experiences, I fought my way into the School of Interdisciplinary Engineering and Sciences (SINES) at the National University of Science and Technology (NUST). With little to no information about the NUST Entry Test, I survived a highly competitive selection — and I was proud of it! But pursuing my dream of becoming a Bioinformatics scientist was not easy either. In a culture where only certain professions like medicine are placed at the top of the job-security hierarchy, my father did not agree to let me pursue my dreams. I sat my competency exams, studied hard, and after one year into my degree — plus a whole lot of persuasion — my father finally agreed to let me be.

Choosing Bioinformatics has been, so far, one of the best decisions of my life. It felt as if I was made for it. Imagine studying 36 subjects, none quite like the other — I have studied more in this one degree than I ever thought possible. It has proven that not only is knowledge a privilege, but fighting for your dreams is absolutely worth it. Studying Mathematics and Chemistry alongside Computer Science and Biology? That would have sounded absolutely crazy to elementary-school me. I may have been overwhelmed at times, but oh, the ride is so worth it!

The Pursuit of Passions and Progression

Passions, to me, have always been widely associated with the arts. I do consider myself a jack of all trades and a master of absolutely none of them — and I wear that title proudly, because I have dipped my hand into several different forms of art and loved every single one.

I was 13 when I started writing poems. Driven by personal pain, it was perhaps a little dark for a teenager to write in such dreadfully raw and aching words — but I continued, and just like the seasons of my life, my words kept changing, growing, and finding new colours.

I sketch, paint, and draw portraits, because holding a pencil in my hand, giving a stroke of brush on canvas has always grounded me — brought me closest to my truest self. There is something almost meditative about it, a quiet conversation between my hand and my heart.

Then there is storytelling, and for that I picked up filmmaking — small projects, one at a time. Through lighting, I discovered I could relay feelings without uttering a single word. How one person stepping out of an elevator can tell you everything about their mood just by the way they walk. How moving a character from darkness into light whispers to the audience that they are reaching for hope and peace. It is a silent language, and I fell in love with it.

Still life found me through my camera — a Canon — where my photography journey first began. I eventually moved on to digicam editing, and ever since, I have made it entirely my own.

And then there is embroidery — perhaps the most soulful of them all. It connects me to my ancestors in the most tender way. In Hindko tradition, women gather in a circle after finishing their morning tasks, pick up their wooden embroidery frames, and breathe life into motifs of Balochi embroidery — stitching clothes, weaving stories, and turning thread into art. Every time I pick up a needle, I feel that circle around me still.

Then there is the art of playing with fire — cooking, and the delicate practice of pouring soul into food. I would not be me without my deep, almost reverent love for cultural food. There is something deeply intimate about the way a dish carries an entire civilization within it — the spices, the technique, the memory baked into every bite. Food, to me, is never just sustenance. It is story. It is identity. It is love made edible.

And the questions food raises are endlessly delicious. Why are noodles the same dish and yet entirely different around the world? From the ramen steaming in a Tokyo alley, to the thukpa warming a Tibetan morning, to the pasta twirling on a Roman fork, to the chow mein sizzling in a Cantonese wok — the same humble strand of dough tells a thousand different stories depending on whose hands shaped it and whose history seasoned it. That single question alone could keep me reading for years.

Or consider how Filipino and Mexican cuisines share flavours so striking, so familiar to one another, that it stops you mid-bite. And then you learn about the Manila Galleon trade route — centuries of exchange between the Philippines and Mexico — and suddenly the similarities are not a coincidence, they are a conversation that has been going on for four hundred years. That is the magic of food. It remembers even when history books forget.

Fusion cuisines fascinate me for exactly this reason — because underneath every fusion dish, if you trace it far enough back, you find a meeting of peoples, a collision of worlds, a moment in history where two cultures looked at each other's tables and said yes, this too belongs to us now. Food does not colonise — it connects.

And that love for story naturally spills into Anthropology — because what is humanity, if not the greatest story ever told? The cradle of Mesopotamia, the grandeur of Muslim history, the vast and layered depths of Asian civilisations, the rise and fall of the world's greatest empires, and the pulse of contemporary history still beating loudly around us — it fascinates me to no end.

What captivates me most is people — their races, their regions, their rituals, and the invisible threads that connect them across centuries and continents. How borders are drawn on maps but cultures bleed beautifully into one another regardless. How every civilisation, no matter how ancient or modern, has looked up at the same sky and tried to make meaning of it.

People are, without a doubt, the most fascinating subject I have ever studied — and I have studied quite a few.
